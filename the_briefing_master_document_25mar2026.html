Israel's deliberate strategy (Middle East Council): The South Pars strike is phase 3 of a deliberate Israeli target sequence: nuclear facilities → military delivery systems → economic infrastructure. By hitting "Iran's wallet," Israel aims to cripple the regime's ability to sustain the war machine. The strategy accepts civilian pain — power cuts, heating disruptions, factory closures — as a calculated cost.

Wood Mackenzie (Tom Marzec-Manser): Even when the conflict ends and Hormuz reopens, Qatari LNG production will not fully resume within weeks. "It could easily take months for nameplate capacity to return, and there will also be an impact on the timeline of the North Field East and South projects."

Qatar's position: Qatar condemned Israel's strike as "dangerous and irresponsible," ordered Iranian security attachés out, and said Iran's retaliatory strikes caused "extensive damage" to Ras Laffan. Qatar is simultaneously a US ally, an Iran neighbour, a major LNG supplier to Europe, and now a victim of both sides. The most complex neutrality position of any country in this conflict.

India's official position: "Strategic autonomy" — neither condemning nor endorsing. India cannot pick sides because it needs all five actors simultaneously. This is not fence-sitting; it is a structurally constrained position driven by hard economic and security dependencies.

The Chabahar paradox: India invested heavily in Chabahar port (Iran) as its gateway to Afghanistan and Central Asia — bypassing Pakistan. The conflict has frozen this strategic investment entirely. Every month of conflict erodes India's Central Asia connectivity position.

The remittance risk: 9 million Indians in the Gulf send $51B/year home. This is not just financial — it is the primary income source for millions of households in Kerala, UP, Bihar, and Rajasthan. A prolonged conflict that displaces Gulf workers would be a social crisis as much as an economic one.

The pharmaceutical clock — India's hidden vulnerability
India PPAC · US FDA · SIA · Gasworld · (Mar 2026)
API inventory buffer
India's active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) buffers are 2–3 months from Feb 28. Late May is the depletion window. Methanol at 87.7% Hormuz exposure feeds the solvent chain for paracetamol, ibuprofen, metformin, and antibiotics.
US generic drug exposure
47% of US generic drugs originate in India. If India's API supply chain breaks down, the shortage becomes a patient access crisis in the United States — not just India. This is a bilateral vulnerability that goes largely undiscussed.
Helium & semiconductor
Qatar supplies 33% of global helium. Helium prices doubled since Feb 28. SK Hynix/Samsung: 6-month buffer. Depletion window: Aug–Sep 2026. South Korea imports 64.7% helium from Qatar. AI hardware supply chain hits a physical wall.

The insurance wall — why a ceasefire doesn't reopen the sea lanes
Solvency II · Lloyd's JWC · Red Sea precedent · Drewry/Xeneta
Ceasefire declared
War-risk premiums remain at peak. Solvency II requires 30–60 consecutive days of zero incidents before P&I clubs begin review. No insurer reinstates on day of ceasefire.
Months 2–6
Gradual JWC reclassification. Spot premiums fall but remain elevated. Shipping resumes cautiously. Red Sea precedent: 26 months and still not normal.
6–16 months
Full normalisation. Markets rally on ceasefire headline — but physical logistics lags by this entire window. The logistics system is the last clock to stop running.
IOR strategic implication: NATO refused Trump's demand to help secure Hormuz. European allies rejected involvement. Japan, Australia, South Korea declined. The vacuum means any naval presence will be US unilateral or — crucially for India — potentially Chinese escort operations for Chinese-bound crude. Greater Chinese naval presence across the northern IOR is now a near-term strategic reality, not a medium-term scenario. This has direct implications for India's maritime security calculus that will outlast this conflict by years.

The transmission mechanism: Oil rises → import bill swells in USD → CAD widens → FX reserves drain → currency weakens → USD debt servicing cost rises → spread widens → yield rises → refinancing wall hits → IMF programme or restructuring.

OECD finding: Egypt and Pakistan face rate differentials close to 5 percentage points between secondary market yields and issuance yields on maturing debt. This was a crisis before the oil shock. Now it is potentially existential.

India's relative resilience: Large FX reserves ($10.2 months cover), mostly local-currency debt (82%), deep domestic investor base. But the absolute scale of the import bill overshoot (>₹4.3L cr annualised) is a fiscal challenge of the first order, and the SPR at 9 days is a critical vulnerability.
